My Journey Out of TUtorial Hell

By Amanda Cutter - March 2023

If you’ve been hanging around the tech world for the last year or two, you have probably seen #100devs at some point. There are more than 46k people in the #100devs discord community at the time of writing this. We are on Discord and Linked In. The #100devs can be seen all over Twitter, and classes can be found on Twitch and YouTube. We are probably secretly trying to take over the world, but that’s just hearsay.

I’m not writing this to brag about or even really talk about #100devs specifically. I want to talk about how I got out of tutorial hell. If you want to know more about why I was stuck in tutorial hell for nearly 15 years, check out my last blog post.

Several things led me to getting out of tutorial hell. While #100devs was a part of it, my first step out of tutorial hell started about six months before I joined #100devs. All in all, four significant things helped rescue me from the grips of tutorial hell to get me to where I am: an opportunity to teach, #100devs, FreeCodeCamp, and moving back to America.

Let me try to explain these without going into too many details. (If you’ve read a few of my other blogs, you know that I can ramble and quickly fill a page or three.)

The Beginning of the End

The beginning of the end of my imprisonment in tutorial hell actually started with a conversation with the principal at the school where I was working. I was complaining about the fact that the school didn’t offer enough computer classes. At the very least, I felt we needed a keyboarding class to teach those kids how to stop pecking at the keyboard whenever they had to type something. It was truly painful to watch.

Eventually, it became one of those “Well, why don’t you do something about it?” situations, and over the next few months, it was decided that I would teach the AP Computer Science A class during the following school year. I am not even going to lie, I was terrified, but the excitement of being able to teach a programming class got me so excited.

I quickly realized that I had a lot to learn because the class was based on Java, and it took a lot of work to learn. Though I had done a C++ programming class five or six years before, I needed to prepare for everything, and I had six months to teach myself before I started teaching some of the most intelligent kids at the school.

Before One Can Teach

Before one can teach, one must learn. I started learning the curriculum that I would be teaching as if I were a student. I really enjoyed it. It was challenging, but that was part of what I loved. However, hints of tutorial hell kept sneaking back into my life, and I found myself shying away from Java and running back to my comfortable HTML and CSS of FreeCodeCamp’s Responsive Web Design course.

But something was different this time. Learning Java and realizing that HTML and CSS aren’t programming languages awoke something inside of me that made me want to know what came next after HTML and CSS for web development. That desire to learn what came next pushed me hard to finish the 300-hour course, and about halfway through my first semester of teaching Java, I successfully completed the Responsive Web Design course that I had actually started for the first time in college.

I wasn’t done, though. Talking to my students about coding, debugging, how coding can help them in college and their future careers, and just seeing their excitement motivated me to keep going. Even though I was still learning Java so that I could teach the students, I wanted to know more about web development, and there was one primary reason why.

Going Back to America

At that point, I was about seven months away from going back to America and living there full-time. I knew that I didn’t want to teach in America, and I was prepared to get a job at Walmart or something to pay the bills until I figured out what I wanted to do next. (Thankfully, better things happened.)

It only took me 15 years from my first programming class to the point of finally realizing that I wanted to be a software engineer. Going back to America, not wanting to teach there, and having the opportunity to teach programming in China all led me to start looking into ways to move further beyond tutorial hell so that I could find a job as a software engineer.

I had a lot of factors working against me. I had two degrees, both in the arts. Nothing to prove that I could be a software engineer except the fact that I was a computer science teacher, but that was only one of the eight years I was teaching. And the only “languages” I really knew and felt somewhat comfortable with were HTML and CSS (markup languages). At that point, I didn’t realize how limited I was, but I knew that getting a job as a software engineer in seven months while still working a full-time job as a teacher of all things was going to be a huge undertaking. (Update: 14 months later, and I’m still not working full-time as a software engineer, but I am getting one step closer every day!)

Final Step to Leaving Tutorial Hell Behind

After looking around at options, wanting to try my best not to get trapped in tutorial hell again, and not having the money to go back to college or pay for an expensive boot camp, I stumbled across #100devs. The creator of it called it an “Agency” that offered training to people who were impacted by the pandemic. You weren’t paid, but you also didn’t have to pay anything.

In other words, I qualified. So, I signed up.

Committed

I don’t know what it was, but there was just something about Leon and #100devs that caused me to be committed to pushing through, to “carry the boats and logs,” as he would say, and to see it through to the end. While I was still in China, that meant waking up on Monday mornings for office hours from 1-3 am every week for the last six months I was in China. It meant waking up and being ready to attend class from 6-9 am on Wednesdays and Fridays, which also meant being fully prepared for my classes because that was my normal planning time.

I, to this day, do not know why I was so driven to do everything that I did as part of #100devs, but I thank God that something pushed me and kept me going even when I was exhausted, stressed, and burnt out.

Present Day

While I still struggle a bit with taking on too many things and running the risk of falling back into tutorial hell, I am at a point now where I know what I want to do. I want to be a web developer. I enjoy full-stack, but I want to focus on front-end development, and I want to make sure that I am marketable.

I still struggle to find balance, but instead of mindlessly following tutorials and doing nothing beyond them, I read the documentation and try to apply things on my own. At the time of writing this, I’m building a new portfolio, working on a project with a friend using Typescript and MUI (two things I’ve never used before), and I’m trying to finish CS50.

Am I still doing too much? Yes, probably. But the difference is that I am learning while doing this. I’m discovering how to do things on my own as a programmer would do on the job. And I’m building and working on projects by myself and with others that I want to do, not because a tutorial said, “Learn React is one hour and build ten React Applications!” or something super unrealistic like that.

So, while I probably rambled a bit more than I had planned to if you made it this far and you have found yourself trapped in tutorial hell, there are ways to get out. Keep fighting. Find people to support you. Fix your focus on one thing, and you will get out one day.

If you are lucky enough not to be drowning in the never-ending tutorials of the web, remember what Leon always says, “Go deep. Not wide.” In other words, find an area of focus and learn as much as you can. Don’t jump from language to language because a Jack or Jill of all trades is a master of nothing useful, left with very little to offer a company when looking for a job.
